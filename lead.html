Data Science Education Program Team Lead Projects

I worked as a Data Science Curriculum Development Team Lead for the Division of Data Science and Information at UC Berkeley for a year and a half, creating assignments (homework, labs, projects) for several courses in development. These are the data science courses I have created assignments for with developers. The courses and their respective Jupyter notebooks are linked in each section.


PH142 - Introduction to Probability and Statistics in Biology and Public Health (Summer 2018)

Number of Developers Led: 1
Description of course: This course is an introduction to statistics and data science, primarily for MPH and undergraduate public health majors, and others interested in public health topics. The course can be divided into three parts: 1) Using R to explore and summarize univariate and bivariate distributions, 2) Central Limit Theorem and Distributions, 3) statistical inference.
Summary of work done: The goal of this lab is to show how to take a sample from a population and how the sample is used to calculate summary statistics to make inference about the population. I utilized birth data for a small state (say Rhode Island) from this link by the CDC. I utilized R to teach students two sampling methods: stratified sampling and the bootstrap and compared the distributions of the birth weights between each method while varying sample size. Finally I gave a note on the central limit theorem to explain why the largest sample's distribution looked normal despite its original distribution of birth weights not being normal.
Link to Jupyter Notebook: https://github.com/ds-modules/PH-142

CP 101- Introduction to Urban Data Analytics (Spring 2019)

Number of Developers Led: 4
Description of course : This course (1) provides a basic intro to census and economic data collection, processing, and analysis; (2) surveys mapping techniques in planning; (3) demonstrates the uses of real-time urban data and analytics; and (4) provides a socio-economic-political context for the smart cities movement, focusing on data ethics and governance
Summary of Work done : Created four Jupyter notebook lab assignments for the course. Soc 130AC- Social Inequalities (Fall 2019)

Number of Developers Led: 2
Description of course: This course explores the causes and consequences of inequality in the U.S. First, we will discuss theories and concepts scholars use to understand inequality. We then consider several institutions that sustain, reproduce and/or mitigate inequality in the U.S., such as education, labor markets, family structure, and the criminal justice system. Within each topic, we pay attention to the significance of race and ethnicity, social class, and gender.
Summary of work done: Students gathered data from different parts of the bay area and entered it onto a google form from their assigned census tracts. We assisted them with exploring the class data and quantify the observations using Python. We visualized how their responses mapped out over the census tracts using the folium library to map their observations onto a map of the census tracts, and include popups with their comments and photos. Next, we sorted the data according to income level. We had students compare the income levels to the level of social disorder and amenities based on the Choropleth visualizations. Lastly, we used linear regression to make a graph that will show the best fit line that correlates to the data.
Link to Jupyter Notebook: https://github.com/ds-modules/SOC-130AC/blob/master/01-The-Neighborhood-fa2019.ipynb
